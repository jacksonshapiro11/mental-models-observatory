### 🔴 CRITICAL POST-PUBLISH ERROR: Earnings Beat Magnitude Mischaracterized
**What happened:** The brief described NVIDIA's Q4 as "crushed," "most decisive beat of 2026," and "every number beat" — language implying a blowout. Actual beat: revenue +2.9% vs consensus ($68.1B vs $66.2B), EPS +5.9% ($1.62 vs $1.53). By NVIDIA's own recent standards (15-20%+ beats in prior quarters), this is a **narrowing** beat. Q1 guidance was stronger (~10% above Street at $78B vs ~$71B), but the brief conflated guidance strength with quarterly beat magnitude.

**Why this matters:** The Take's central thesis — that the market's muted response to NVIDIA is the interesting signal — is actually *stronger* with accurate framing. A modest beat + flat stock = the market is digesting, not ignoring a blowout. The hyperbolic language ("crushed") undermined the brief's own best insight. Worse: if a reader checks the actual beat percentage, they lose trust in every other number in the brief. Credibility is the only asset we have.

**Post-market outcome:** NVIDIA sold off after hours and tech sold off broadly the following session. This makes the mischaracterization worse — the brief framed a ~3% beat as a "blowout" while the market was already telling us it wasn't enough. The Take's thesis about the market's muted response was directionally correct, but the premise ("historic beat met with a shrug") was wrong. It wasn't a historic beat. It was a narrowing beat that the market correctly read as a deceleration signal. The sell-off confirmed what accurate language would have implied.

**Root cause:** Writer and editor both accepted "beat = big" without checking beat *magnitude* against consensus estimates and NVIDIA's own beat history. The editor's price verification caught ETH and silver errors but did not verify the *characterization* of earnings beats — only the raw numbers. The critic rated The Take as Essential without flagging the language mismatch.

**What must change:**
1. **New editor check: "Earnings beat characterization."** For any earnings report, verify: (a) actual vs consensus numbers, (b) beat percentage, (c) how that compares to the company's recent beat history. "Beat" and "crushed" are different claims.
2. **Language discipline rule:** Never use "crushed," "blowout," "demolished" for beats under 5%. Reserve superlatives for genuinely exceptional prints. A 3% revenue beat is "solid" or "modest." A 10% guidance beat is "strong." Words have to match data.
3. **Critic must check Take framing against underlying data.** If The Take builds a thesis on the market's reaction, the critic must verify the premise. "Market shrugged at a historic beat" is a different thesis than "market shrugged at a modest beat with strong guidance." Both are interesting — but only the second is accurate.
