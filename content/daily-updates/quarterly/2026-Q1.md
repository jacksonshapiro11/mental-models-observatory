Week 1: our Take mapped the second-order chain. By week 4: every link confirmed. Hormuz closed → insurance cancelled → tankers stopped → storage overflowed → production cut → force majeure declared. Iraq's involuntary force majeure was the definitive signal — this isn't a voluntary disruption with an off-ramp. It's an involuntary one that compounds. Total Gulf disruption: 5-6M bpd, 1973-scale.

# ▸ THE QUARTERLY TAKE

### The Regime Changed in Q1 — Here's the New Map

Every quarter has a narrative arc, but most are gentle. Q1 2026 broke. The regime that governed markets from mid-2024 through February 2026 — AI-led growth, gradual rate normalization, manageable geopolitical risk — ended on February 28. What replaced it is a war economy with three defining characteristics:

**1. The dominant macro variable shifted from monetary policy to commodity supply.** For two years, every market conversation started with the Fed. As of Q1-end, every conversation starts with oil. This matters because monetary policy has a clear transmission mechanism (rates → credit → growth) with a known decision-maker (the Fed). Commodity supply disruptions have no clear transmission mechanism when they're involuntary. There's no decision-maker. Iraq can't decide to reopen Hormuz. The war's oil impact propagates through physics (storage capacity, pipeline routes, refinery throughput), not policy.

**2. The market is running two parallel economies.** The AI economy and the war economy coexist but operate on different logic. AI capex is accelerating because it's become a national security imperative — the spending survives the war because it has to. Consumer/cyclical spending is deteriorating because oil at $112 is a tax on everything. Micron posting 31% earnings beats while the S&P makes new lows is the two-economy thesis in a single data point. Q1's lesson: you can be right about both economies and still lose money if you're in the wrong one.

**3. The feedback loops are self-reinforcing.** Oil disruption → inflation → hawkish Fed → dollar strength → gold liquidation → margin calls → more oil speculation → more disruption pricing. Each variable amplifies the others. Self-reinforcing loops don't have natural stopping points — they require an exogenous shock (ceasefire, massive SPR release, Saudi production surge) to break. None are visible.

**What this means for Q2 and beyond:**

Q2 will be the quarter where the data catches up to the regime change. March PCE (released late April) will be the first inflation reading that fully reflects $100+ oil. Q1 earnings will reveal which companies adapted to war-economy conditions and which are still planning for the old regime. The Fed's June meeting will be the first where rate hikes are a serious discussion, not a tail risk.

The historical analog that fits best is Q4 1973 – Q1 1974: oil embargo shock, followed by a 3-month lag before the economic data confirmed what the market had already begun pricing. The lag exists because economic data is backward-looking while markets are forward-looking. We're in that lag now. The market knows the regime changed. The data hasn't confirmed it yet. When it does, the repricing accelerates.

**Where this framework might be wrong:** If a ceasefire materializes (both sides rejected, but Trump has reversed before). If Saudi Arabia opens non-Hormuz routes at scale. If the war ends quickly through a decisive military outcome. Any of these would break the self-reinforcing loop. None are base cases, but they're non-zero.

# ▸ INNER GAME — QUARTERLY REFLECTION

*A quarter of daily practices distilled into what actually helps.*

Twenty-five Inner Games covered ground from Buddhist equanimity to Bowlby's attachment theory, from Paul Farmer's systems compassion to Pema Chödrön's radical groundlessness. The themes that kept recurring:

**Presence is not passivity.** The instinct during volatile markets is to grip harder — more screens, more alerts, more scrolling. Every Inner Game pushed the opposite direction: presence with what's actually in front of you, not anxiety about what might happen next. The people who navigated Q1 best were the ones who could hold two realities simultaneously — awareness of the macro AND presence in their daily lives.

**Connection stabilizes cognition.** Bowlby's research kept showing up because it's the most practically useful framework for market volatility: securely attached people think better under stress. Your portfolio decisions in Q1 were shaped more by the quality of your relationships than the quality of your analysis. This isn't soft advice — it's neuroscience.

**Systems > blame.** When your thesis loses money because oil margin calls forced gold liquidation, that's a system event, not a personal failure. Farmer's insight — that the system determines outcomes — applies to portfolios as much as public health. Understanding the system that produced the loss is more useful than blaming yourself for being in the position.

**Quarterly practice:** Write down the three most important relationships in your life. Not the three most important positions. Not the three most important predictions. The three most important people. Are you investing in them with the same attention you give your portfolio? If not, you've identified where Q2's real returns come from.
