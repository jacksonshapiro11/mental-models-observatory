## ▸ THE UPDATE

**The Market Rallied on a Number That Expired Before It Printed**

June CPI fell 0.4%, twice the expected decline, pulling the annual rate to 3.5%, and the S&P took it as permission to rally. But the softness has one driver: gasoline fell about 10% in June because a Hormuz ceasefire had reopened the shipping lanes. That ceasefire collapsed on July 8, crude has since rallied 30%, and the blockade is back. So the print is a photograph of a month that ended before the war resumed. The structural read is a calendar mismatch. Energy flows into goods prices on a one-to-two-month lag, which means today's crude is next month's core inflation. The August 12 CPI is the first print to carry a full month of the reinstated blockade, and it reintroduces the exact input June just subtracted. The tell is simple. Watch whether crude holds near current levels through July. If it does, the soft June number is the last easy inflation read the market gets, and the 10-year at 4.58% will look in hindsight like the final quote before the repricing.

**The Toll Lasted 22 Hours. The Blockade Never Moved.**

Trump announced a 20% levy on all Hormuz cargo Monday evening. By Tuesday afternoon it was gone, replaced with Gulf investment pledges after allied navies, the UN maritime agency, and Gulf states hosting US bases all pushed back. The speed is the signal. A unilateral toll on an international waterway is indefensible under maritime law and unenforceable without allied ships, and the allies said no inside a single news cycle. The blockade of Iranian vessels, reimposed the same Monday, needs no one's consent and is still in force, backed by nightly US strikes on Iranian coastal defenses. One instrument was a trial balloon; the other is a military fact. The oil market graded them correctly, giving back only $2 of the rally when the toll died and holding the other $19. The forward read sits in the budget. A third of Trump's $87.6 billion war supplemental, roughly $29 billion, is earmarked for sustained Hormuz operations, which is the government's own forecast that the blockade outlasts the headlines. Watch whether that money is spent on escort capacity or quietly reallocated.

**The Banks Just Printed a Record Quarter as Hedge Funds, Not Lenders**

Combined Q2 net income across JPMorgan, Bank of America, Goldman, Wells, and Citi cleared roughly $49 billion, up 39% year over year. Almost none of it came from lending. JPMorgan's equity-trading revenue jumped 86%, Goldman's earnings per share nearly doubled to $20.98, and investment-banking fees surged on the SpaceX listing. Net interest margin, the number that measures the actual lending franchise, sat flat near 2.36%. That is the whole story: the record was built on the most cyclical, lowest-multiple revenue a bank earns, while the compounding engine investors actually pay a premium for went nowhere. The precedent is close. Q4 2021 ran the identical script, record trading on the SPAC boom, and by Q2 2022 dealmaking revenue had fallen 50%. Trading beats do not compound; they mean-revert. Watch the next quarter's mix. If the beat is again trading against flat lending margin, the market is paying a franchise multiple for hedge-fund earnings, and that gap closes the moment volatility does.

**Google Cut Off Meta's AI Models Because It Ran Out of the One Thing Money Cannot Buy Fast**

Google stopped supplying Meta with its Gemini models after Meta requested more capacity than Google could spare, the first confirmed case of one hyperscaler rationing AI access to another on pure capacity grounds. The structural read: when the two biggest AI spenders on Earth cannot satisfy each other, the binding constraint is no longer capital or talent but physics, specifically the advanced-packaging slots at TSMC that stitch these chips together. TSMC's June revenue jumped 68% year over year, and it reports Thursday at 2 AM Eastern with consensus near $40 billion and gross margins above 65%. That call is the tell for the next six months of AI hardware. Watch the packaging-capacity guidance. If TSMC signals demand outrunning even its expanded plans, the bottleneck tightens on everyone from Nvidia down to the in-house silicon programs at Google, Amazon, and Meta, and the AI-spending-ceiling debate shifts from whether to how fast.

**The Clearinghouse Everyone Wanted to Disrupt Just Ate the Disruption**

DTCC, which settles essentially all US securities, begins limited production trades of tokenized equities, ETFs, and Treasuries this month, with full service in October, backed by a consortium of more than 50 firms including BlackRock, Goldman, JPMorgan, and Circle. For three years the tokenization pitch was disintermediation: public blockchains settling instantly and routing around the clearinghouse. The go-live inverts it. The incumbent absorbs the technology, so instant settlement accrues to the party that already owns the rails. History rhymes. The electronic networks that promised to kill the exchanges in the 1990s ended up bought by them. Tokenized stocks have crossed $1 billion in value and 185,000 holders, so the growth is real; the only open question is who captures it. Watch whether independent tokenization platforms keep their volume once DTCC is live, or whether flow migrates to the entity that already clears $2.4 quadrillion a year. The rails usually win.

**Oncology's Hottest Drugs Are Rationed by Atoms Only Seven Reactors on Earth Can Make**

Radioligand therapies, cancer drugs that carry a radioactive isotope straight to the tumor, are oncology's fastest-scaling category, and Novartis's Pluvicto alone did about $2 billion in 2025, up 42%. But the active ingredient cannot be scaled with a bigger check. Lutetium-177 comes from roughly seven high-flux research reactors worldwide on weeks-long runs, and the more potent actinium-225 is so scarce that global supply covers on the order of 1,000 treatments a year. TerraPower broke ground on a $450 million plant to lift output twentyfold, and analysts already expect it to fall short by 2030. The choke point is upstream. China controls the feedstock for lutetium-177 and added it to its export-control list, with enforcement suspended only until November 2026. Watch that date. If China implements the control, or any major radioligand drug lands back on the FDA shortage list, the value migrates from the drug developers to whoever owns the scarce inputs and the cold-chain logistics.

## ▸ INTERESTING THINGS

**Tau, the Alzheimer's Villain, Turns Out to Be What Makes Memories Stick**

Researchers at the Salk Institute found that tau protein, long treated purely as a marker of Alzheimer's pathology, is essential for turning new experiences into lasting memories. It stabilizes the microtubule scaffolding that ferries molecular cargo to synapses; without it, new memories form but never consolidate. The finding reframes tau from a disease flag into a load-bearing part of how the brain records a life. (Salk Institute, *Neuron*, July 2026)

**A 200-Year-Old Trick Fresnel Used to Prove Light Is a Wave Just Built Exotic Light**

A team at Nanyang Technological University created optical skyrmions, three-dimensional light structures that had only been theorized, using the Poisson spot, the same phenomenon Fresnel invoked two centuries ago in the argument that settled light's wave nature. The structures are topologically protected, meaning they resist perturbation, which makes them candidates for optical data storage that does not degrade. (NTU Singapore, *Nature Photonics*, July 2026)

## ▸ THE MEDITATION

*"To study the Way is to study the self. To study the self is to forget the self. To forget the self is to be enlightened by all things."*
– Dōgen Zenji, *Shōbōgenzō* (*Genjōkōan*, 13th century)

You would assume self-improvement requires more self-knowledge: a sharper catalogue of your strengths and weaknesses, an ever more detailed map of who you are. Dōgen says the opposite. The destination of self-study is not a more elaborate self but a self so thoroughly known that it becomes transparent, and what comes through the transparency is everything else.

The difference is felt, not theorized. There is a version of working on yourself that makes you more self-conscious, not less: more watchful of your reactions, more careful about your image, the internal monologue getting louder instead of quieter. That is studying the self without forgetting it, and the people around you feel it as heaviness, someone always monitoring, never simply present.

The opposite state is the one where your effort has become so thorough it no longer needs your attention. The musician who drilled scales for ten thousand hours and now improvises without thinking about her fingers. The parent who read every book on listening and now simply listens. The skill became part of the structure rather than part of the performance. That is what Dōgen means by forgetting: not carelessness, but the moment self-knowledge stops being a project and becomes a floor you stand on without looking down.

**Today's practice: pick the one self-improvement effort you have been monitoring most closely this week. Today, do it without checking on it. No journaling, no measuring, no noting how it went. Just do it, and let the doing be the whole record. If by tonight you cannot remember whether you did it well, that is the sign it has started to become structure rather than performance.**

## ▸ THE MODEL

### Somatic Markers: Why the Body Knows Before the Mind Does

In the early 1990s, neuroscientist Antonio Damasio studied patients with damage to the brain region linking emotion to decision-making. Their IQs were intact and their logic was sharp, yet they made catastrophic decisions across finances and relationships. The Iowa Gambling Task exposed why. Given decks of cards, some rigged for ruin, healthy players started sweating over the bad decks before they could consciously explain the pattern, their bodies learning the game before their minds caught up. Damasio's patients never got the signal and kept drawing until they went broke. He called these body-level tags somatic markers: compressed emotional residue from past experience, delivering a verdict before deliberation finishes. The tool: when a choice feels wrong despite looking right on paper, name the past experience the feeling is compressing, then ask whether it still applies.
